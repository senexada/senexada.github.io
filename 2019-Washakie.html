Washakie Wilderness
August 2019


We have seen great vistas, but for continuous scenery, hour after hour, this year was the best yet. A picture is worth a thousand words, so let's go.

Oh, the Washakie Wilderness is in Shoshone National Forest (Wyoming) and constitutes part of the massive southeast Yellowstone roadless area. As usual, click photos for high res version.




Day One

We began with some foreshadowing: the ranger's only comment about Kirwin was "there's bears up there;" it was sprinkling and 47 degrees at 10 am (Aug 23rd); the trailhead parking lot was vacant; and the trail was faint from the very start. Excellent signs!

Sadly, the biggest fail occurred before leaving the parking lot, when Padraig assessed his sinuses against the weather and decided to abort. Given our soaking over the next day, it was the right call. When he re-enters the story, his own adventures will add texture and irony to our tale.

Parking lot: only us

The trail up Brown Basin climbs through a bit of forest that quickly opens into meadow & tundra. Jason was the pace car, despite hauling fresh produce and both frying pans. The flat light allowed great photos amidst sprinkles of sleet and rain.

Brown Basin "trail"

Above the tree line

We ate lunch atop Greybull pass, the first 11k' pass of our trip, while looking north into the massive Greybull valley and headwaters.

Greybull Valley from Greybull Pass

After a 15-minute descent across the steep, loose, slope, we entered miles of gentle grasslands with barely a trail to be found.

Descent from Greybull Pass. Glad we're heading down, not up

Getting gentler

Ah, meadow heaven

The drenching rain began as we passed the old Miner's cabin near Yellow Creek. Alex sat in the ruins for a bit.

Rain descending Yellow Creek above the cabin

The next hour was wet and cold. After fording the Greybull at the Steer Creek confluence, we continued a bit farther before choosing a spot near the river, past the first stream on our right. Thankfully the rain stopped before dinner, though everything remained damp til morning. As usual, we cooked our Wagyu Ribeye and cornucopia of vegetables; the bacon brussels sprouts were particularly tasty this year.

Sunset on the upper Greybull

Day Two

Dawn was frosty and damp. Starting a campfire at 645am required some cheating. Alex did the usual.

Bacon by fire

By 9am we finally had warm sunshine. We became fully dry for the first time in 24 hours.

Continuing downstream, we reached the Cow Creek junction, with its knee-deep crossing to turn up Cow Creek.

Greybull ford at Cow Creek

The first hour afterwards had flat, easy hiking through a pungent meadow, hopping across the creek once or twice.

Up Cow Creek

After lunch it veered left into the best continuous valley we've seen in years: switchbacks up a massive bowl with elk roaming near the ridge. The trail often disappeared, or nearly so.

Getting better

Us (specks in the grassy middle) and elk (specks in the snowfield)

We rested atop gentle Burwell Pass, the second 11k pass of our trip. The skies had that special clarity of one-day-post-storm. Aside from gusty winds, conditions were ideal.

Near Burwell Pass, looking back from whence we came

Burwell Pass, looking east

The descent briefly follows the ridgeline before dropping onto a quality trail, comfortably etched into the grassy slope.

Ridgeline descent

Excellent trail down from Burwell Pass

Within an hour we reached the valley floor. We set camp a few minutes downstream and ate our deliciously salty and carby jambalya while the clouds rolled in. Around the campfire we reflected that when integrating over an entire day, today's hike was perhaps our all-time best, given the hour after hour of ever-changing, beautiful vistas.
