Global Warming

Large historic variability before fossil fuels

  • CO2 Concentration - has varied from 200 - 5000+ ppm [2, p201]. Current level is 400ppm.
  • Polar Ice - during most of the earth's history the poles have been ice-free [1, p38]
  • Temperature - Before the end of the Mesozoic era climates were substantially warmer than today [1, p39]
  • Glacier Retreat - The Scandinavian ice sheet retreated at the rate of about 1km per year between about 10,000 and 9000 years ago [1, p154]
  • Temperature - The last postglacial thermal maximum was reached about 6000 years ago, and climates since then have undergone a gradual cooling. [1, p181]

[1] National Academy of Sciences / National Research Council, "Understanding Climatic Change," 1975, ISBN 0-309-02323-8, full text from internet archive (accessed Nov 1, 2019)
[2] Berner & Kothavala (Yale), "Geocarb III: A Revised Model of Atmospheric CO2 Over Phanerozoic Time," Feb 2001, American Journal of Science Vol 301 p.182-204, Full text (accessed Nov 1, 2019)

Model failures

  • IPPC 1990: average global-mean temp increase of 0.3C per decade (uncertainty range 0.2 - 0.5C per deacde) [3, p.73] Actual: 0.14C/decade from 1990 to 2018 [4]
  • Glacier National Park 2017: computer models show all glaciers will be melted by 2020 [5] Actual: still glaciers

Global Cooling in 1970s

1) NOAA Magazine, Oct 1974, Volume 4 Number 4

  • Text Excerpt - Many climatologists have associated this [prolonged African] drought and other recent weather anomalies with a global cooling trend and changes in atmospheric circulation which, if prolonged, pose serious threats to major food-producing regions of the world.
  • Text Excerpt - There is reason to believe, for example, that rice was once grown far north of its present boundary in parts of Asia.
  • Text Excerpt - Some climatologists think that the present cooling trend may be the start of a slide into another period of major glaciation, popularly called an "ice age." Many other scientists disagree. J Murray Mitchell Jr [...] a world authority on climate change, comments, "We observe these trends, and we know they are real. But we can't find the central tendancy; we just don't know how long they will last." Mitchell himself suspects that the present cooling trend will reverse itself rather soon.
  • Text Excerpt - In summary, the current cooling trend may be but a temporary climatic variation, it may lead to another "Little Ice Age," or it may, indeed, signal the death of the present interglacial period. We do not know. We may never know. Whatever the case, a study of the past reveals that the Earth's climate is highly variable - indeed, that variability is one of its fundamental characteristics.
