Yosemite
October 2024


Only two of last year's eight attendees made it to Yosemite this year for a glorious but warm autumn in the Sierra. What were the upsides and downsides? Read on to see.



The Arrival

I arrived Fresno 8pm with only carry-on luggage (first time attempting it; simply gave up on packing a knife). Bill picked me up, we slept in Fresno, and were driving north to Wawona at 624a on Thu Oct 3.

Along the way we discussed my new equipment this year: the KS Ultralight pack. It weighs one pound and has a minimal frame. Way lighter than an REI pack, just as comfortable, yet also cheaper than REI, despite being custom hand-sown by a sole proprietor in one of the costliest places on earth (Japan). Which begs the big-box retail question explored by Flight of the Conchords in Think About It: "What are your overheads?"

The ranger station at Wawona Hotel was empty (arriving half hour after they opened, we dodged the 8-person opening rush). The ranger surprised us with his bear advice, so different than the northern rockies: sleep *near* your bear cans; act aggressively toward bears, as if the bear is a big raccoon; but don't stone them to death, which accidentally happened in a Lord of the Flies frenzy recently (oops). He also surprised us with the forecast: close to 100 degrees at Hetch Hetchy.

A juvenile bear skittered across the parking lot as we arrived at White Wolf at 10a. We took our starting photo and began the dirt road walk toward Harden Lake.

White Wolf parking lot

After passing Harden Lake (not particularly scenic), we admired the trailsmanship of the switchbacks down the south rim of the Grand Canyon of the Tuolumne. The first few miles had no veiws; at one point we got a few glimpses of the deep blue waters of Hetch Hetchy Reservoir (but only Bill's phone has that photo?); as we got lower, the views pointed toward Pate Valley and Rancheria Mountain.

Rancheria Mountain

It was an unusual backcountry entry: a net 3500' of elevation *loss* as we descended from the Tioga Road highlands into the furnace of Tuolumne canyon. Along the canyon floor we took multiple breaks to soak our shirts in the river.

Soaking shirt in the Tuolumne

We crossed the two good bridges in Pate Valley and found a shady campsite in the tall pines. It was dark by 7:15p, which foreshadows our biggest challenge of this October trip: staying awake until a reasonable bedtime.

Day Two

We woke about 620a to maximally benign conditions: about 60F, no wind, no moisture anywhere, not even a hint of dew. It is so easy to manage gear like this, compared to last year's constant hail/rain/snow in Wyoming.

The only "problem" was our ham coldcuts intended for lunch, which had been at ambient 60F+ for the last 12 hours. We reasoned that they were probably fine, but better to sizzle & eat now than wait another 6 hours at daytime temps approaching 90. Weeks later we consulted the most reliable part of current year internet -- the message boards -- which verified that we were indeed still alive and unharmed. (Feel free to skip the blockquotes if uninterested in bacterial toxin production)

Quora thread
Vegetative cells (e.g. E. coli, Salmonella, etc.) are all destroyed by proper cooking, just as you do with poultry. No toxin-producer affects raw meat in 8 hrs. The only thing that grows rapidly on raw meat is SPOILAGE organisms.
Stack Exchange thread
For example. at 10C (50F), E. coli only manages to divide once every 8 hours or so (see Ratkowsky et al., "Relationship Between Temperature and Growth Rate of Bacterial Cultures", J. Bacteriology, vol 169, p. 1 (1982)

Edit: In response to a comment about bacterial toxin production, I want to reiterate that colder temperatures slow down metabolism of just about everything, including toxin production. This is because, at a basic physical level, reaction rates are governed by the Arrhenius equation which translates, for simple reactions, to a doubling of reaction rates for ~10C increase in temperature. Of course, organisms like bacteria have more complex interactions, but this still gives an order of magnitude estimate.

Furthermore, research has been done on production of bacterial toxins. For example, Skinner & Larkin (J. Food Protection vol 61 p. 1154 (1998)) wrote a paper called "Conservative Prediction of Time to Clostridium botulinum Toxin Formation for Use with Time-Temperature Indicators To Ensure the Safety of Foods", which gives, for food innoculated with the bacteria, a time-to-detection-of-toxin of 2-3 days at 10C. In fact, they did the research because food storage at open-face refigerators in stores often allows products to get up to as high as 10C (at least as of 1998).

Similarly, in Bonventre and Kempe ("Physiology of Toxin Production by Clostridium botulinum Types A and B, III"), their 10-18C toxin line is flat for 24 hours at the baseline level before creeping up by a factor of 3 or so between 24 and 48 hours (figure 4). These are just examples, but you find the same general trends everywhere because of the fundamental physical relationship between reaction rates and temperature.

Walking by 812a, we hoped to ascend the +3500' while it was mostly shaded. The angle of the sun and color of the leaves made it feel like autumn.

Morning in Pate Valley

Upward toward Pleasant Valley

It took 3.5 hours (including all breaks) of steady climbing to reach the trail junction to Rodgers Canyon. This section had no water except a tiny seeping stream at the junction, barely enough to harvest. We couldn't find the pond it supposedly drains. We ate lunch atop the ridge with a stiff breeze and quaking aspens.

Lunch near junction 7780

The next two hours were pretty standard Sierra terrain, mostly downhill, but including a long hot stretch through what felt like a rolling sea of granite knolls. We left the trail at Piute Creek, going upstream through some dead pine forest followed by a nice ledge scramble toward Saddle Horse Lake. We erred right and cliffed out near the Lake Irwin stream, which travels through an impassable gorge. Then we retreated and soon reached the saddle at the foot of Saddle Horse Lake.

Conquered the ledges along Saddle Horse creek

Next was a short but intense bushwack through dense foliage and deadfall along the west side of Saddle Horse Lake. Within five minutes we found game trails, which we followed to an aspen-lined meadow on the northwest side near its head. It felt like no one had visited in a long time; there were no obvious trails, campsites, fire rings, or signs of use. We settled among some boulders, cooked jambalaya, and watched the pastel California sunset.

Campsite NW of Saddle Horse Lake

Day Three

The night was lovely, dark, and deep -- new moon for the win. It was also much cooler, probably about 40F. We watched the valley come alive with chirps, calls, and rustling as the low October rays filtered through the aspens about 8am. I think of aspens as a Colorado & Utah tree; it was rewarding to see them here, on the verge of turning yellow.

Campsite at dawn

Last night we decided to do an easy day hike to allow flexibility for a more efficient exit, yielding time for a shower and In'n'Out. We planned to scout the north shore of Saddle Horse Lake and climb the hill on its southeast side.

I love the contrast of these trips. Today we had warm, gentle, dry October sun without an insect or a hint of moisture, not even dew. Last year on day three in Wyoming we had rain, multiple hail storms, and high winds. Each is inspiring in its own way.

Our short day hike was pretty brushy and ledgy.

Unnamed hill SE of Saddle Horse

It offered nice views north toward Benson Lake and the PCT (neither visible here). Pleasant Valley is not an ordinary "valley" in the sense of having a clear valley floor. It is surrounded by peaks, but instead of an obvious floor there are ridges and gorges and knolls that limit the sight lines and disrupt the sense of place.

Looking North from high point near Saddle Horse

We observed our off-trail travel speed varying by something approaching 10x. Smooth, flat rock was as fast as on trail, while scrambling through underbrush, ledges, and deadfall could take an hour for each 1/3 to 1/2 mile.

Bill at midday on Saddle Horse Lake

Our short hike finished by 1115a and rather than stay another night, we lounged at the lake and then backtracked a couple miles to Table Lake. The ledges were much easier on the way down: when routing away from the dry creek and westward, there were nice ramps between the ledges. As the terrain flattened, we reached a large work camp (currently uninhabited) that didn't offer any views, but did offer some peaceful cascading pools sized for swimming.

Pools along Piute Creek

Back on trail at Piute creek, we recrossed the hot, strange topography then scouted for Table Lake, which we found within 3 minutes of leaving the trail. Finding the optimal campsite along the south shore took a few more minutes. The map shows some marsh & swamp, but we had a nice dry site with no detectable marsh or bugs. It was really quite a fabulous mix of dry, flat ground, open skies, and just enough foliage to feel comfy and protected.

Table Lake in Pleasant Valley

We had another beautiful night: still, dry, and perfect temperature. We built a fire, watched the long dusk, and managed to stay awake until after nine (quite a challenge when the sun sets before seven).

Sunset at Table Lake

Day Four

After our coldest night yet (still 40F at 8am) we woke to our first morning with moisture, just enough dew to delay our early departure and lend time to enjoy daybreak on the lake.

Daybreak on Table Lake

We hiked modestly uphill to the junction, then began the countless switchbacks down 3000'+ to the canyon floor. We had some early shade in a unusual scrub oak forest, with leaf litter on trail that felt almost eastern.

Oak forest on the switchbacks

Soon we were in full sun, hotter by the step, with heat shimmering off every granite wall. We encountered our first person since day one, a solo hiker on a week-long trek.

Down into the heat

We stopped to eat and swim in the wonderfully cold water of the Tuolumne at a swimming hole beneath the bridge. This was the first "busy" part of our trip, with six people passing as we ate, and another few as we hiked down valley.

Two bears, a mother and cub, were foraging 10 yards from the trail and largely ignored us until I tried to get a photo, at which point they showed enough interest that I chose to walk away. We continued through the last bits of fast, level, shady trail.

Classic sierra forest, Pate Valley

Then it was several hours of sweaty, steep switchbacks.

Upward

We debated whether to stop at Morrison Creek, to continue to Harden Lake, or to hike all the way to the car at White Wolf. By 430p we had hiked about 12 miles, up and down 3000'+ of vertical, in intense heat, which was plenty for me. We found a great hollow below the trail, made ramen, and watched the clouds & colors develop over the Grand Canyon of the Tuolumne.

Retrospective

The two-man trip was a nice variation -- numerous campsite options, easy decision-making, and lots of conversation time.

Yosemite delivered classic sun-washed granite, gentle evenings, glorious sunsets, and essentially no bugs. The low elevations along the Tuolumne (4400') were uncomfortably hot, but the higher elevations were perfect. The alternate year Cali vs northern Rockies contrast increases one's appreciation of both sets of conditions.

Traveling in October was a nice variation. I liked the changing leaves and low angle of the sun. The notorious Yosemite crowds were absent: the entry gate (on Thu morning) had only three cars in line; the ranger station was empty; White Wolf trailhead had only a couple cars; and we only saw about a dozen people in the backcountry, and aside from two people near the trailhead, the rest were confined to two brief time windows in Pate Valley. We saw no one on three full days and large majority chunks of days 1 and 4.
