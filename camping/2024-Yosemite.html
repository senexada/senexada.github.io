Yosemite
October 2024


Only two of last year's eight attendees made it to Yosemite this year for a glorious but warm autumn in the Sierra. What were the upsides and downsides? Read on to see.



The Arrival

I arrived Fresno 8pm with only carry-on luggage (first time attempting it; simply gave up on packing a knife). Bill picked me up, we slept in Fresno, and were driving north to Wawona at 624a on Thu Oct 3.

Along the way we discussed my new equipment this year: the KS Ultralight pack. It weighs one pound and has a minimal frame. Way lighter than an REI pack, just as comfortable, yet also cheaper than REI, despite being custom hand-sown by a sole proprietor in one of the costliest places on earth (Japan). Which begs the big-box retail question explored by Flight of the Conchords in Think About It: "What are your overheads?"

The ranger station at Wawona Hotel was empty (arriving half hour after they opened, we dodged the 8-person opening rush). The ranger surprised us with his bear advice, so different than the northern rockies: sleep *near* your bear cans; act aggressively toward bears, as if the bear is a big raccoon; but don't stone them to death, which accidentally happened in a Lord of the Flies frenzy recently (oops). He also surprised us with the forecast: close to 100 degrees at Hetch Hetchy.

A juvenile bear skittered across the parking lot as we arrived at White Wolf at 10a. We took our starting photo and began the dirt road walk toward Harden Lake.

White Wolf parking lot

After passing Harden Lake (not particularly scenic), we admired the trailsmanship of the switchbacks down the south rim of the Grand Canyon of the Tuolumne. The first few miles had no veiws; at one point we got a few glimpses of the deep blue waters of Hetch Hetchy Reservoir (but only Bill's phone has that photo?); as we got lower, the views pointed toward Pate Valley and Rancheria Mountain.

Rancheria Mountain

It was an unusual backcountry entry: a net 3500' of elevation *loss* as we descended from the Tioga Road highlands into the furnace of Tuolumne canyon. Along the canyon floor we took multiple breaks to soak our shirts in the river.

Soaking shirt in the Tuolumne

We crossed the two good bridges in Pate Valley and found a shady campsite in the tall pines. It was dark by 7:15p, which foreshadows our biggest challenge of this October trip: staying awake until a reasonable bedtime.

Day Two

We woke about 620a to maximally benign conditions: about 60F, no wind, no moisture anywhere, not even a hint of dew. It is so easy to manage gear like this, compared to last year's constant hail/rain/snow in Wyoming.

The only "problem" was our ham coldcuts intended for lunch, which had been at ambient 60F+ for the last 12 hours. We reasoned that they were probably fine, but better to sizzle & eat now than wait another 6 hours at daytime temps approaching 90. Weeks later we consulted the most reliable part of current year internet -- the message boards -- which verified that we were indeed still alive and unharmed. (Feel free to skip the blockquotes if you uninterested in bacterial toxin production)

Quora thread
Vegetative cells (e.g. E. coli, Salmonella, etc.) are all destroyed by proper cooking, just as you do with poultry. No toxin-producer affects raw meat in 8 hrs. The only thing that grows rapidly on raw meat is SPOILAGE organisms.
Stack Exchange thread
For example. at 10C (50F), E. coli only manages to divide once every 8 hours or so (see Ratkowsky et al., "Relationship Between Temperature and Growth Rate of Bacterial Cultures", J. Bacteriology, vol 169, p. 1 (1982)

Edit: In response to a comment about bacterial toxin production, I want to reiterate that colder temperatures slow down metabolism of just about everything, including toxin production. This is because, at a basic physical level, reaction rates are governed by the Arrhenius equation which translates, for simple reactions, to a doubling of reaction rates for ~10C increase in temperature. Of course, organisms like bacteria have more complex interactions, but this still gives an order of magnitude estimate.

Furthermore, research has been done on production of bacterial toxins. For example, Skinner & Larkin (J. Food Protection vol 61 p. 1154 (1998)) wrote a paper called "Conservative Prediction of Time to Clostridium botulinum Toxin Formation for Use with Time-Temperature Indicators To Ensure the Safety of Foods", which gives, for food innoculated with the bacteria, a time-to-detection-of-toxin of 2-3 days at 10C. In fact, they did the research because food storage at open-face refigerators in stores often allows products to get up to as high as 10C (at least as of 1998).

Similarly, in Bonventre and Kempe ("Physiology of Toxin Production by Clostridium botulinum Types A and B, III"), their 10-18C toxin line is flat for 24 hours at the baseline level before creeping up by a factor of 3 or so between 24 and 48 hours (figure 4). These are just examples, but you find the same general trends everywhere because of the fundamental physical relationship between reaction rates and temperature.

Walking by 812a, we hoped to ascend the +3500' while it was mostly shaded. The angel of the sun and color of the leaves made it feel like autumn.

It took 3.5 hours (including all breaks) of steady climbing to reach the trail junction to Rodgers Canyon. This section had no water except a tiny seeping stream at the junction, barely enough to harvest. We couldn't find the pond it supposedly drains. We ate lunch atop the ridge with a stiff breeze and quaking aspens.

Lunch near junction 7780

The next two hours were pretty standard Sierra terrain, mostly downhill, but including a long hot stretch through what felt like a rolling sea of granite knolls. We left the trail at Piute Creek, going upstream through some dead pine forest followed by a great ledge scramble toward Saddle Horse Lake. We erred right and cliffed out near the Lake Irwin stream, which travels through an impassable gorge. Then we retreated and soon reached the saddle at the foot of Saddle Horse Lake.

Conquered the ledges along Saddle Horse creek

Next was a short but intense bushwack through dense foliage and deadfall along the west side of Saddle Horse Lake. Within five minutes we found game trails, which we followed to an aspen-lined meadow on the northwest side near its head. It felt like no one had visited in a long time; there were no obvious trails, campsites, fire rings, or signs of use. We settled among some boulders, cooked jambalaya, and watched the pastel California sunset.

Day Three

The night was lovely, dark, and deep -- new moon for the win. It was also much cooler, probably about 40F. We watched the valley come alive with chirps, calls, and rustling as the low October rays filtered through the aspens about 8am. I think of aspens as a Colorado & Utah tree; it was rewarding to see them here, on the verge of turning yellow.

Campsite at dawn

Last night we decided to do an easy day hike to allow flexibility for a more efficient exit, yielding time for a shower and In'n'Out. We planned to scout the north shore of Saddle Horse Lake and climb the hill on its southeast side.

I love the contrast of these trips. Today we had warm, gentle, dry October sun without an insect or a hint of moisture, not even dew. Last year on day three in Wyoming we had rain, multiple hail storms, and high winds. Each is inspiring in its own way.

Our short day hike was pretty brushy and ledgy.

Unnamed hill SE of Saddle Horse

It offered nice views north toward Benson Lake and the PCT (neither visible here). Pleasant Valley is not an ordinary "valley" in the sense of having a clear valley floor. It is surrounded by ridges & peaks, but there is no clear floor. Instead there are ridges and gorges and knolls that limit the sight lines and disrupt the sense of place.

Looking North from high point near Saddle Horse

We observed our off-trail travel speed varying by something approaching 10x. Smooth, flat rock was as fast as on trail, while scrambling through underbrush, ledges, and deadfall could take an hour for each 1/3 to 1/2 mile.

Bill at midday on Saddle Horse Lake

Our short hike finished by 1115a and rather than stay another night, we lounged at the lake and then backtracked a couple miles to Table Lake. The ledges were much easier on the way down: when routing away from the dry creek and westward, there were nice ramps between the ledges. As the terrain flattened, we reached a large work camp (currently uninhabited) that didn't offer any views, but did offer some peaceful cascading pools sized for swimming.

Pools along Piute Creek

Back on trail at Piute creek, we recrossed the hot, strange topography then scouted for Table Lake, which we found within 3 minutes of leaving the trail. Finding the optimal campsite along the south shore took a few more minutes. The map shows some marsh & swamp, but we had a nice dry site with no detectable marsh or bugs. It was really quite a fabulous mix of dry, flat ground, open skies, and just enough foliage to feel comfy and protected.

Table Lake in Pleasant Valley

We had another beautiful night: still, dry, and perfect temperature. We built a fire, watched the long dusk, and managed to stay awake until after nine (quite a challenge when the sun sets before seven).

Sunset at Table Lake

Day Four

After our coldest night yet (still 40F at 8am) we woke to our first morning with moisture, just enough dew to delay our early departure and lend time to enjoy daybreak on the lake.

Daybreak on Table Lake

We hiked modestly uphill to the junction, then began the countless switchbacks down 3000'+ to the canyon floor. We had some early shade in a unusual scrub oak forest, with leaf litter on trail that felt almost eastern.

Oak forest on the switchbacks

Soon we were in full sun, hotter by the step, with heat shimmering off every granite wall. We encountered our first person since day one, a solo hiker on a week-long trek.

Down into the heat

We stopped to eat and swim in the wonderfully cold water of the Tuolumne at a swimming hole beneath the bridge. This was the first "busy" part of our trip, with six people passing as we ate, and another few as we hiked down valley.

Two bears, a mother and cub, were foraging 10 yards from the trail and largely ignored us until I tried to get a photo, at which point they showed enough interest that I chose to walk away. We continued through the last bits of fast, level, shady trail.

Classic sierra forest, Pate Valley

Then it was several hours of sweaty, steep switchbacks.

By 430p we had hiked about 12 miles, up and down 3000'+ of vertical, in intense heat, which was plenty for me. We found a great hollow below the trail, made ramen, and watched the clouds & colors develop over the Grand Canyon of the Tuolumne.

The team at Scab Creek Trailhead

Looking backward (southwest), toward the plains

The local wood was not so dry and it took 1.5 hours til the coals were ready for cooking. Starting our appetizers around 6:30, the last steaks came off the fire around 8:00, just as the sun was falling behind the ridge.

Day Two

The rain finally came before dawn -- we were nearly in a fog, with a gentle pitter-patter that induced my best sleep of the night. John and Bill were already awake when I got up at 6:30. Though still cloudy and damp, it didn't rain during the breakfast taco prep, and we even got a few bits of glorious sun around 10:00. The dampness made for really slow packing, and we weren't walking until after 11:00.

Taking the south trail around Dream Lake required a lengthy and dicey rock-hop, which all survived. Dream Lake was a slate mirror in the foreboding light.

Steve above Dream Lake

Within minutes the foreboding turned into actual boding (?) -- a vigorous 15 minutes of hail. Yet by minute twenty it broke and we could see blue sky again in the west. What glorious and powerful skies.

Beginning to clear on trail 7094

This stretch -- along trail 7094, then off-trail between Dream and Raid Lakes -- was one of our top all time meadow walks, reminiscent of Siberian Outpost in Sequoia National Park. The open meadows were framed by craggy peaks and wild unsettled skies -- and no people.

Toward Bonneville

We saw one section hiker as we briefly crossed the CDT (Continental Divide Trail), a veritable highway in these environs, though only 18 inches wide. Then we followed the clear and quality usage trail along the north side of the stream that drains Bonneville lakes. The valley has some thick willows, so the trail was essential for quick travel. It delivered us to an outstanding spot on Bonneville Lake 10521: a lake fed by waterfalls and nestled in a 2000 foot deep valley. We had the whole place to ourselves: we saw no one on this stretch, or really all day, except for the one hiker on the CDT.

Day Three

I slept poorly due to the foam mat on rocky ground, and thus was awake early enough to beat even Bill, who seems to always be wandering around camp before I'm moving. The two waterfalls and some chirping birds were the soundtrack to the dusty pink dawn behind the peaks. Moments like this are why I do it.

Dawn at Bonneville

There are times -- when cold, exhausted, with altitude headache, sleeping poorly, neck aching -- when I wonder if it's worth it. Then God provides a morning like this. I walked 18k steps yesterday and 27k the day before, just five months after breaking my kneecap, and I've had no pain. The past two days had numerous throwbacks to prior years: Dancing Bear wine, like our first night in Glacier; the Highline above Dream Lake, like Siberian Outpost; this morning's pink dawn, like East Lake in King's Canyon; the trout fishing, like we had hoped for in Golden Trout. The setting was magical for reflection.

John, too, in the pensive mood

I wore every piece of clothing during pancake time. The sky was cloudy and gusty and unsettled. It simply felt cold. Rain seemed imminent but again spared our breakfast. Then Bill, John, Steve and I departed up-valley for a day hike to Sheila Lake. It felt destined to rain (or worse) at any moment, but the day hike wasn't going to hike itself.

Ten minutes into the hike, as we finished a talus field on the north side of our lake, the hail arrived in earnest. We each sheltered in various shrubs from the pea- to marble-sized hail. When the worst had passed, we resumed travel and found Steve, who had run ahead in shorts and t-shirt, shivering in a copse of trees. The wind chill was probably in the 20s when the front blew through.

Wind River: a winter wonderland, even in August

The scene back at camp while we were day-hiking

The clouds lifted as quickly as they had dropped, until they settled on the ridgetops. We resumed the journey to the uppermost Bonneville lake. We didn't find any clear trail after this point; we scrambled up the ledges on the north side of the stream.

Toward upper Bonneville

Water break at upper Bonneville, with a great cloud ceiling

We had some mist on this section, but nothing drenching. After a long rocky traverse of the Bonneville lakeshore, then a gentle climb, we reached Sheila Lake. Steve scrambled a good ways up the snowfield.

Steve above Sheila Lake (south side)

We returned along the north shore of upper Bonneville and were surprised to find three hikers sunning at its outlet. We said hello and crossed to the south side of the outlet, thinking it would be gentler than the ledges we had scrambled to get up the valley. But it turned out to be thick with willows and almost certainly slower & more difficult, both in the upper and lower reaches.

John descending south side of stream

The ridge offered a good view of our camp, our lake, and its islands. It took an hour from here to return to camp.

Our lake & camp

The skies foretold more wind and rain, and we didn't want to get fooled again. So we built quite the fire.

This big fire sure feels good

Episodes of wind and rain interrupted dinner; at one point two people held a tarp over Suhas as he shaped the pizza dough. It took about three hours to cook all the pizzas -- but they were amazing, and we ate them during the golden hour when a bit of welcome sunshine appeared.

It was a great day. The day hike was interesting but not too hard. It was magical to experience the hail storm and the mountains shrouded in mist.

The final discussions were about -- you may have guessed -- bombing out the next day. The cold winds, rain, hail, and snow had softened our party's resolve. A wet morning would almost certainly slow us enough to dissuade an early exit, but if the morning proved dry, who knew what would happen. An interesting wrinkle is that I had genuinely lost the best map on day two.

Day Four

The morning was misty. We cooked oatmeal in light rain. All gear was wet so an early departure -- and thus early exit -- seemed unlikely.

Then at 10:00 glorious sunshine appeared and the drying commenced. The motivated members were packing and walking in 20 minutes, with the rest of us trickling out after. We made a rapid descent to the CDT, then retraced our off-trail route between Dream and Raid lakes.

Retracing the delightful meadow near Dream Lake

The first major meeting was deciding whether to return to Crescent Lake, to take an established but circuitous trail past Crescent, or to take the gentle off-trail terrain toward the trailhead.

This way? Or that way?

We decided to head southwest, off-trail, toward trail 7110. It was a good choice: direct, easy terrain, and only one small stream crossing. As we touched trail 7110 we passed an old cabin with a phenomenal view.

Someone loved this spot long ago

And shortly after, we had one of our last clear views back toward the continental divide.

As we continued downhill in the forest it began to rain -- first a little, then a lot. We had more hail and a little snow, then steady rain for an hour. The trail was a mess. Surprisingly we saw quite a few groups of people and stock heading inbound.

Miles of muck

We had skipped lunch. By 2:20 those of us in the rear reached the huddled, drenched, cold, and hungry leaders. Having stopped moving for a few minutes, they were near shivering. And they had voted already without us. Which kind of makes sense for a wet, shivering crowd. There was no compelling reason to set camp in these conditions.

So we walked another four hours, reaching our cars at 6:15, having taken 38k steps for the day (and my knee: still good). And true to the Winds, the weather changed again: skies were clear and dry by the end.

Retrospective

The Winds have a wild reputation that we witnessed firsthand: furious rain and hail storms, and even snow, interleaved with gentle sunny moments. Several times I was the coldest I've been in years: wearing every piece of clothing, yet damp and on the verge of shivering. The running shoe is suboptimal in those conditions. The rain and wind prompted another early exit, but the trip was nevertheless a success, with plenty of memorable and brilliant episodes.

The Winds also have a reputation for summer crowds and summer mosquitos, both of which we largely avoided. The busiest stretch was from the trailhead to the meadows, where we passed multiple parties of people and stock in both directions. On the middle days we were mostly alone, with no stock and very few people. Getting off the officially-maintained trails seems to do wonders. The Great Outdoor Shop told us that we got lucky on the mosquitos, with a freeze that killed them early this year. It would be fun to explore this area after Labor Day, though doing so would require more cold-weather preparation.
