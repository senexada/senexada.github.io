Golden Trout & Sequoia, Sep 2011

Of all the mountain ranges I have climbed, I like the Sierra Nevada the best […] Benevolent, solemn, fateful, pervaded with divine light, every landscape glows like a countenance hallowed in eternal repose

John Muir, 1901

We return to the eastern Sierra at yet another trailhead: Horseshoe Meadow.  As usual, clicking the images should get you the high res versions.  And prepare yourself for a long post.

—–

Given its proximity to major cities, the size of the Sierra wilderness is inspiring.  Our three recent trips, spread 25 miles apart, shared the same contiguous roadless area. Traveling its long dimension, we’d need to go another 90 miles to hit the first road, Tioga Pass in Yosemite.  And those are straight line distances.  Traveling an actual footpath (the John Muir trail) requires 211 trail miles with 80,000 ft of elevation change.

Even Tioga pass is a seasonal road: six months of the year you must travel another 100 miles, nearly to Lake Tahoe, before crossing the first passable road. Granted, this is the long dimension.  The width varies between 20-40 miles, which gives several thousand square miles of rugged, roadless wilderness.

—–

Despite missing John and (still) Anit, we gathered a party of 8.  And gathered great ambitions. We aimed to transcend freeze dried food in favor of steaks and sausages.  We aimed to catch those famous golden trout for a supplemental meal.  We aimed to do a lot of off-trail travel.  We aimed (some of us) to reach Kern hot springs.  Were our aims accomplished?  Partly.

Day one could only be called an unqualified success.  Drove across Death Valley to Lone Pine by mid morning.  Drove the first 16 miles into the wilderness along the solitary spur leading to Horseshoe Meadow.  Hit the trail around high noon:

Suhas, Steve, Roger, Jeff, Alex, Padraig, Bill, Jason

The trail starts gently and climbs an easy 1200′ to Cottonwood Pass, the southern-most 11k’ pass along the Sierra crest.  We saw a handful of day hikers and through hikers on the PCT, the last people we would see for 4 days, aside from two 2-minute encounters during the initial 24 hours.

Cottonwood Pass

We hiked quickly down the 1200′ to the western edge of the deceptively large Big Whitney Meadow.  Several miles across, the sense of scale is hard to comprehend (note the people in the center of the photo):

Across Big Whitney

Jason spotted some finger-sized trout at the first stream crossing, raising our hopes for the latter streams and the lakes to come.  After crossing to the far side of the meadow we chose a campsite along Golden Trout creek, still with plenty of time before dusk.  The luxury of it!  200 miles of driving, 8 miles of hiking, a gentle 1200 vertical feet, our own subalpine meadow, and daylight to spare for dinner.

And the luxury was just beginning.  Dinner itself was a masterpiece.  Ribeye steaks cooked medium rare on an open fire, plus spinach and risotto balls.  For a first backcountry meal, Suhas exceeded all expectations.  Even the coyotes couldn’t keep quiet.

Dinner prep

Ribeye!

The moon set around 11pm and the previous immensity of stars grew more staggering yet.  Satellites & meteors appeared by the minute while the Milky Way shone bright and clear.   Most of us slept outside; the starry heavens, as they have for millennia, filling man with endless wonder.  Day one: flawless victory.

—–

Day two began with a rosy sierra dawn and breakfast burritos — with real eggs.  The eggs (perhaps the sausage?) got the coyotes howling again.  Gold star for Jason.  We started the mild climb on the seldom-traveled trail toward Rocky Basin.

To Rocky Basin

We stopped below Rocky Basin Lakes for lunch, soon turning west for the off-trail travel to Johnson Lake, which was benign:

To Johnson Lake

We passed two people leaving the lake — our last encounter for about 3 days — and found a great site on the south shore.   The water was brisk but inviting and crystal clear.  Most of us swam.

Johnson Lake

Sadly, there was not a fish in sight, but the casting was elegant:

Fly fishing

One ranger had mentioned a fish “removal” to protect an endangered frog, but we didn’t expect all the fish to be gone.  Yet they all seemed to be gone.  Last year at Kearsarge Lake we could sit a few minutes at dusk and see hundreds of fish jumps. This year, nothing.  Jason remained hopeful for a Funston Lake trip the next day, as it’s inside Sequoia NP and thus is managed differently.

Dinner, again, was spectacular.  You might wonder how raw sausages could last until the second night in the backcountry?  Our secret: Alex.  The man carried 5 pounds of carbon dioxide 15 miles through mountain terrain so we could eat sausage.  Plus full potatoes.  The magnanimity of this feat cannot be overemphasized.  Potatoes. And dry ice. For two days.  The dry ice containment system was his own invention, too.

Sausage & hashbrowns

Night fell, we slept early, and we entertained our fancies for tomorrow, the “off” day.

Fishing at dusk

—–

Day three.  Our camp would remain here another night.  We could relax at the lakeside, summit the nearby peaks, scout the lakes within Sequoia, or attempt the rumored off-trail descent to Kern hot springs.  The last was the glimmer in my eye: one of the most remote hot springs in the contiguous states, about 20 trail miles from the nearest road, nestled over 4000′ below us in the Kern River canyon, and according to this post (the first 7 entries, from 2010), hikeable from our location.

The crew was justifiably skeptical of a 4000’+ off-trail descent of a canyon wall, but Steve was willing.  He and I set off at 8:30am for what would become the toughest hike I’ve ever done (I’m not even sure if it made Steve’s top 3).   Roger and Padraig made some key contributions to our pack and we left, thinking we’d be home about dusk if it went well, or a few hours later by headlamp if it didn’t.  Meanwhile, the others relaxed at camp and explored Funston Lake and the nearby portions of Sequoia.

Jason made some phenomenal photos of the Boreal Plateau:

Boreal Plateau

and Funston Lake:

Funston Lake

Earlier that day, Steve and I had passed Funston Lake on the way into Kern Canyon.  We caught our first glimpse of the vertical drop into Kern river canyon (with the Great Western Divide beyond it):

Kern River Canyon from afar

I wrote a lengthy reply (see the messages starting Sep 2011) to the original forum post that inspired our route, but lest that thread vanish, I’ll cover the relevant points here.  There’s one narrow section that’s safe to descend, which we overshot to both the south and the north, wasting several hours.  We ate lunch near the shoulder of valley, where it steepens, around 1:30pm:

Kern Canyon

Having finally found the hikeable gullies, we descended the final ~2000′ in about two hours, finally reaching the river:

Made it to the Kern

We waded the river (the trail runs along the far wall) and found the rest of the canyon to be a swamp.  The hot springs were so close — only a mile or two of flat terrain — but we only had 4 hours of daylight and Steve’s foot was bruised, so we had to turn back.  We had a lot of vertical to ascend.

Noting the scarcity of water on our descent, we chose to ascend the unnamed creek that drains Funston Lake.  After re-crossing the swamp, enduring dozens of mosquito bites, two separate bee-swarming incidents, and re-wading the Kern, we started up the creek around 5pm.

I have no photos from the ascent.  It was extremely difficult.  The next 9 hours were nearly continuous hiking and scrambling, from 6800′ up to 11200′.  When the sun set, we hiked by moonlight; when moon set, we hiked by headlamp.  Steve got blisters on all 10 toes.  We both had episodes when the climb seemed insurmountable.  We ran out of food and water.  It got cold.  We had fire-starting equipment, but we knew that if we didn’t return to camp by dawn that our friends would report us missing (per our plan) to the Rock Creek ranger station.

Around 2:00am we reached the final descent to Johnson Lake and saw headlamps from afar.  Within the hour we reached camp.  Everyone got up to start a fire, make us drinks, etc.  Steve & I were pretty wiped (the GPS showed 7000′ of ascent throughout the day):

3am return

Roger told me later that he woke up every hour to check if we had gone into shock.  Thank you to him and everyone.

—–

Day four.  I actually felt pretty good — another credit to last night’s reception and the liquids they forced into us.  We hiked from the Johnson basin up into Sequoia park, following the route that some of us had scouted yesterday.

Hiking up into Sequoia NP

The elevations in Sequoia are higher than the tree-covered Golden Trout wilderness, and from Boreal Plateau we could see the granite slabs of the Sierra crest.

Sierra crest from the Boreal Plateau

Jason, who’d run ahead to fish the lakes in the western Siberian Outpost, took a great shot of us walking the ridge between Rocky Basin lakes and the outpost. We are the ants on the center of the ridge (might need to click for high res to see us):

The ridge above Siberian Outpost

The fishing was another fail.  No trout to be found, and no idea why.

After lunch we crossed the spring-fed meadow and hiked the deceptive length of the outpost to the far eastern side

Across Siberian Outpost

More Siberian Outpost

Even more Siberian Outpost

where we set our final camp on the 11k’ meadow and ate pesto pasta.  Our bodies must have needed carbs: everyone had seconds, and some people thirds.

Our final night was again under an immense sky of stars, satellites, and meteors.  Living these days outdoors — sleeping under the stars, waking at dawn, absorbing the California sun, hiking miles of empty terrain — promotes a vigor and healthfulness that I think John Muir had in mind when he wrote of the mountains’ appeal to “tired, nerve-shaken, over-civilized people.”

—–

Day five. After another indescribably pastel California dawn

Dawn on the Outpost

we packed our final camp

Final morning in the mountains

and hiked out.

Leaving Sequoia

We exited on the PCT, past Chicken Spring lake to Cottonwood pass.  Looking back on our goals, the food was a major victory: the best backcountry meals I’ve ever had.  The fishing was a fail:  the so-called golden trout wilderness, devoid of trout?  The off trail travel to Johnson and across Siberian Outpost: superb, and all to ourselves.  And Kern hot springs: fail, but a spectacular one.

Other notes of personal interest for future trips:  real meat tasted great, but don’t forget the carbs; the gravity water filter was a workhorse; GPS was helpful after dark, particularly in UTC mode; and mylar blankets work wonders.

Until next time.
