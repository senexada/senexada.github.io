Bob Marshall Wilderness
August 2016

Who is Bob Marshall? Why have I never heard of him or his wilderness? Where is this place? Queue the back story.

It begins with one of the genuinely useful publications of our general government: a map of distance to the nearest road in the lower 48 (original pdf here).

Courtesy of USGS

The algorithm is simple: find the biggest dark green spots we haven't visited. Future candidates include the Olympics & Cascades (WA), Frank Church (ID), and Yellowstone complex (WY). This year we chose the largest green blob in Montana, which happens to be called the Bob Marshall complex. It is a 1.5 million acre wilderness that was named after an early conservationist.

Oddly-named wilderness areas remain obscure for reasons I don't understand but I do enjoy. Compared to national park backcountry, the scenery is comparable, the permits easier (often nonexistent), the camping and fire rules friendlier, and the crowds smaller. For background, a wilderness designation prohibits roads, permanent structures, and mechanized travel, with only a few grandfathered exceptions. About 5% of the lower 48 states (110 million acres) are officially wilderness, which does not include non-official areas that happen to be roadless.

If you think "The Bob" is a strange name, the naming gets even stranger. The Bob's marquee feature, a 1000 foot cliff that extends unbroken for 20 miles along the continental divide, is called the Chinese Wall. With a description and name like that, we had to see it.

Montana is outrageously beautiful. The rolling prairies and mesas, dotted with more cattle and horses than people, surrender abruptly to forest at the foothills.

Near Augusta, MT

At one point we had to stop for three steer occupying the road, and those steer outnumbered the cars we saw in the hour between Augusta and Benchmark. The end of the road had a well-marked trailhead, a paucity of cars, and a luxurious outhouse.

I'm becoming increasingly partial to the northern Rockies. They have rivers and green undergrowth. The trails are wild and scenic but not too high or steep or dry for a first day arrival from sea level carrying steaks, bacon, sausage, and eggs.

We entered along the Continental Divide Trail (CDT). It was gently rolling, well trodden, with several bridges, allowing rapid travel into the backcountry with modest exertion. The view from the first pack bridge, a few hundred yards from the car, was pure Montana.

Getting straight to business: the South Fork Sun

The trail goes north through shady forest, yielding to burn scars in the lower valley, yielding to open meadows west of the confluence.

Above the South Fork and West Fork confluence

It then follows the West Fork Sun, sometimes low in a meadow, sometimes high enough for a view.

Low in a meadow

High enough for a view

Thanks to our earlyish start we traveled 11 miles and made camp at 4pm just before the Ahorn trail junction. We had hours of sunshine to cook the steaks and spinach and assemble the fresh kale salad. We should strive for more early starts.

Backcountry kale

We felt great. I had no overexertion from a steep climb, no altitude problems (we were at 5800'), and no dehydration. There was no wind sapping our heat. We had a campfire to cook ribeye and to keep us up after sundown.

Dehydrated food is for chumps
