Absaroka Beartooth Wilderness
August 2022


Twice in this series we have exited the backcountry early: once for wildfire smoke, once for a confluence of heavy winds and route challenge. This year was our third early exit, for a new reason to be revealed to the perservering reader. As usual, you may click images for a high-res version.


Day One

Bozeman was our launch point into the 944,000-acre Absaroka Beartooth Wilderness, which lies along the north border of Yellowstone. We departed the downtown Kimpton (brand new) on Saturday morning Aug 6 after a superb avocado toast on site. Driving by 7:40am, we took I-90 east to Livingston, exited onto Swingley Rd, and lost cell service by 08:25. We hit the National Forest boundary at 09:00, heading into the valley amid an unsettled forecast and clouds that threatened rain.

Southbound on Boulder Creek Rd, with rain threatening

Road quality was excellent, and the road had been repaired from a major recent washout, presumably due to the many large permanent camps along this stretch. It degraded only for the last handful of miles, during which we averaged 8-10 mph, reaching Upsidedown Trailhead (our exit point) at 09:58 and Box Canyon Trailhead (our starting point) at 10:10. A few spots felt sketchy in our SUVs, so we were humbled to see six horse trailers and a Corolla were among the ten vehicles at the trailhead.

It was about 50F and verging on rain as we packed. We got the group shot at a fine bridge right at the parking lot.

The Start

The trail up East Fork Boulder Creek began wide and gentle, quickly entering the tall pines.

An easy stroll into the clouds on East Fork Boulder Creek

The rain started about noon and continued most of the day and night, so the photo above is all you get today. The handful of unforested sections had nothing to see due to the rain and mist. But the trail was gentle, the creeks were well-bridged, and the forest was glistening, so it was a pleasant hike nonetheless.

Ok, I lied, I found one more unspectactular photo before the rain

We couldn't see much in the mist, but we passed a few erratic boulder fields interrupting the forest, and saw the small lake after the Rainbow Creek junction, which was pretty and had a campsite.

We continued up Rainbow Creek to a decent campsite (or so it seemed in the mist) at about 8800'. We managed with great effort to burn the drenched wood in a meager fire, just enough to cook the steaks. Despite several heavy bouts of rain, which soaked every sock I was carrying, everyone was satisfied with the day: good trail, good distance, good elevation, reasonable temperatures, and few mosquitos. There wasn't much lounging after dinner, as the rain continued most of the night.

Day Two

At 8:30 the glorious sun reached our camp. I spent a couple hours drying my entire wardrobe, and others did similarly. We broke camp at 11am and by noon I felt hot and dry, a short six hours after feeling that everything I owned would be damp the entire trip. We declared a "0%" chance of rain for the rest of the trip.

Night One Camp on Rainbow Creek, starting to dry out

We passed a handful of groups today after seeing only two people in yesterday's rain. Ascending toward Lake Plateau, we encountered the first meaningful meadows and open vistas.

Up Trail 28, approaching Lake Plateau

At Wounded Man Lake we passed two fisherman. Continuing along its north shore on trail 93, there was deadfall across the trail and accordingly no sign of recent stock usage. We took a break at the foot of Lake Pinchot before moving onward toward our target of Ravin (or maybe Cimmerian) Lake.

North shore of Woulded Man or Pinchot (not sure which) from Trail 91

The trail after Pinchot became narrow & overgrown as it steeply descended the forested ravine. Dreary Lake is aptly named and had no decent campsites, due to sloped forest or marshy ground everywhere we scouted.

After Dreary, we lost the trail as it braided through a few small meadows. Ultimately we found it again at a bridge across the stream near Lake Ravin. Ravin was beautiful, but we scouted all the way to Ravin foot without finding a large enough campsite. We considered scouting onward to Lake Surrender, but ultimately surrendered even sooner and worked our way back upstream toward Pinchot. There was a decent site north of the trail on the west end of Ravin, but the views weren't as good as the foot of Pinchot, so we continued to retrace our steps upstream.

Around 5:30 we reached the beautiful meadow at the foot of Lake Pinchot, having traveled 9.5 miles today (27k steps according to my watch).

Decided: camp on NE shore of Lake Pinchot

We ate Ramen and S'mores and began to battle the mosquitos, which will figure prominently over the next two days. The headnets were well worth their 1oz weight. Any exposed skin, or skin beneath a single layer, was attacked. As it cooled enough to don multi-layer mosquito protection we watched twilight over the lake.

Lake Pinchot, looking west at dusk

Total solar energy normal to incidence at our date/location

On the topic of day hike, despise the clouds of mosquitos, the only takers for a stroll above treeline (mosquito free?) were Bill and me -- oh Jason, where art though? We targeted the unnamed mountain south of Lake Pinchot and west of Clam Lake. Heading southbound along the west shore of Dreary Lake required a significant traverse through a talus field.

Long stretch of loose rock above/west of Dreary Lake

The traverse was not too bad on the way up, yielding good views of Dreary and Ravin Lakes.

Dreary (near) and Ravin (far) lakes from slope above Dreary

I spotted Trouble Lake on our ascent but didn't capture a good photo. There were some interesting rock slabs near its outlet. I never caught a good look at Clam Lake, due to the topography and forest.

We reached the unmarked summit after about two hours of hiking. Despite the rocky terrain and high winds, there were... mosquitos.

Looking west to Lake Plateau from near the summit

Due to the flat mountain top, we had to move slightly downhill and north to see Lake Pinchot. Our campsite is not quite visible, even on the high res version, due to iphone lens limitation. Diaphonous Lake and the tiny unnamed lake above Pinchot looked attactive, as did the nice hikeable-looking ramp from there to Chalice Peak.

Lake Pinchot and Chalice Peak

This was a solid day trip: good views, good distance, good challenge. We basically retraced our route on the descent. At one point of the talus slope we diverged and found ourself among car-sized to house-sized boulders, which made for slow and fatiguing down-climbing.

Upper portion of descent; looking down at Lakes Dreary & Pinchot

We found the stationary folks to be stationary and bemoaning the mosquitos. It was difficult to swim without numerous bites.

The backcountry pizza collaboration was great, as usual, with half of them cooked with our masterly wood-fired grilled pizza technique. This dinner is a true backcountry treat. (But Suhas says: always remind him about the glutard and lactard).

Culinary achievement. Note the headnets.

It was another peaceful evening with 0% chance of rain. Donning multiple layers to defeat the mosquitos, we watched the orange twilight over Pinchot.

Day Four

Today's plan was to travel about halfway back toward the cars, crossing Lake Plateau and camping at Horseshoe Lake. I had learned my lesson from prior hikes and placed us 16 miles from the road to dissuade the annual agitation for early exit. (If we had camped at Ravin Lake it would have been 17+. But even from Pinchot, I figured 16 would be adequate).

I underestimated the attractive & repulsive forces: the attraction of post-hike Vegas (for the usual suspects) and the repulsion of the swarms of mosquitos. Early-exit agitation began. Even so, 16 miles would dissuade the agitators, right? Especially with our late start at 1040am.

Leaving Pinchot, where we had seen no one for two days, we encountered over a dozen people on Lake Plateau, several at each lake or trail segment. One fisherman told us the lakes freeze to the bottom in winter, and that the fish are air dropped by helicopter (is that believable?). The plateau itself is gentle and framed by distant mountains.

Across Lake Plateau

The Rainbow Lakes were pretty and had plenty of flat, open ground for camping.

One of the Rainbow Lakes on Lake Plateau

About 1pm we reached the junction with Upsidedown Creek Trail (junction of 28 & 26) and began the great debate: follow the plan (camp at Horseshoe Lake), pivot to an early exit down Rainbow Creek (retracing our entry), or hike to Horseshoe Lake, eat lunch, and re-evaluate. After some dubious advice from a bypasser, and a dubious vote-counting exercise, we chose the third option.

Small saddle above Horseshoe Lake

Horseshoe Lake was... pretty good. Absent mosquitos & Vegas, the original plan may have prevailed. But as the debate circled without progress, and the clock approached 4pm, Padraig's ten simultaneous bites put him over the edge, and he made a unilateral departure toward the cars.

Horseshoe Lake

I disliked the situation. I was unsure if everyone was up for a 16 mile day, with a potentially joint-jarring 3000'+ of descent from Horseshoe to the road. And now we were separated into several groups without a plan.

The physical exertion proved not too bad: the trail was good and the switchbacks were gentle. There wasn't much water and my bottles ran dry with five miles to go. I perhaps had a salt imbalance, because I had clear urine but insatiable thirst, which was cured when Bill fed me salt. Several rockslides made this route more pleasant by opening views of the healthy forest and by making the trail impassable to stock.

Looking back up-valley from a rockslide area

And that's about it. We completed the early exit on day 4 by covering the last 7 miles in about 3.5 hours, bringing the day's total to 16.5 miles. We were driving, with various levels of energy and mood, at dusk.

Retrospective

Scenery was good on the middle days. There was decent foot traffic on Lake Plateau, but we had solitude beyond Wounded Man Lake. Hiking a day in the rain had its upsides: fewer bugs and fewer people. Ultimately mosquitos and Vegas colluded for an early exit, despite the stationary site being 16 miles from the road. I guess I need to plan an even longer route next year.
