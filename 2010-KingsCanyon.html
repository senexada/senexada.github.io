Kings Canyon, Sep 2010

Three years after our Mt. Whitney travails we plotted our return.  Too late for the Whitney lottery, and wanting more solitude anyway, we looked north to the Onion Valley (Kearsarge Pass) entrance to Kings Canyon backcountry. [All photos in this post should be linked to high res versions if you click them]

The 2007 crew was only half present: Jeff & Alex.  Padraig had a wife pregnant enough to preclude him going 4 days off the grid.  Anit, for reasons of his own, chose Dave Matthews over the high sierra.  P & A were replaced by 3 others: Jason, triathlete; Bill, inappropriate guy; and John, carrier of 5 compasses and buyer of the book “Finding your way without map or compass.”

I get sick at altitude, mostly migraines.  This trip would be my longest, highest backcountry trip to date — 30 miles, all above 8400′, and sleeping between 9500-11000′ .  Hydration hasn’t worked very well in the past, nor has sleeping.  So I asked the internet what I’m doing wrong.  The internet said: eat & drink more carbs.  I planned meals with (# of grams of carbs) = 5 * (# grams fat + protein).  I drank about 800 calories of carbs a day.  I think it helped, but two notes for next time: 1) I coveted & had to borrow other people’s meat & cheese; 2) my stomach & teeth starting missing regular water, especially after 2 days.

Fully loaded packs weighed in from 34-44 lbs, without water.  John’s was the lightest thanks to cost being no object.  Alex’s was the heaviest, thanks to… whatever Alex brings.

We left Vegas Saturday at 7am.  Lone Pine came quickly, where we saw the famous Grizzly Adams of last trip’s fame.  He said everything was awesome and the East Lake stream was too high to cross.  It’s like an invitation!  The weather forecast on a whiteboard showed the daytime highs:  Death Valley 119F, Mt Whitney summit 46F.  They’re about 50 miles apart.

North to Onion Valley road, pretty standard curvy road up to the lot at 9200′

Onion Valley

On the trail at 1245pm, Jason quickly & clearly in the lead.  The rest of us doing fine until John got the dreaded wet cough, which slows you down.  Standard eastern Sierra terrain: rocky & exposed & always upward.

Westwar toward Kearsarge

Covered the 5 miles, 2700′ to Kearsarge Pass in reasonable time.  The rightmost notch is the pass, 11845′.

K Pass in sight

The haze is from a forest fire burning in the western part of the park.  Mornings were clear and afternoons brought the smoke.  We rested at the pass; John & Bill ate their Bouchon delicacies; we decided to go another 5 miles, -2300′ to camp at Lower Vidette Meadow.  After the initial descent into the park, the next 2 miles were gentle:

Kearsarge Basin

And had that magical California evening glow.  After joining the John Muir trail southbound we dropped quickly into the forest below East Vidette Peak:

East Vidette

And found the meadow at dusk.  Pumped water in the dark, cooked simple meals, & slept under the stars.  The night was cold — near freezing or below.  Morning light gave our first good view of the meadow, our sight, and the surrounding peaks:

Campsite night 1

Jason, God bless him, cooked bacon.  Bacon!  At 9500′, 9 miles from the nearest road.  We could all learn something from the kind of guy who carries bacon that far.

At breakfast the temp was still colder than it looked.  The first rays of sun over Kearsarge Pinnacles coaxed Jason & Bill (and the rest of us in turn) into the sun.  I have never felt sunshine more warm and welcome than the California sun above a frosty or snowy high sierra valley.

Vidette Meadow at Sunrise

Onward toward East Lake (the one behind the impassible stream, if you recall).  First was a 2.5 mile descent along Bubbs Creek to Junction Meadow.  You start to get the idea why they call it ‘Kings Canyon’ (note Alex in the lower right)

Kings Canyon

We rested a bit past the twin pines and developed some movie script ideas.  The easy descent got the creative juices flowing, I guess.

At Junction Meadow we turned south toward East Lake.  Almost immediately we found the impassible stream, which proved about as impassible as we expected — meaning, impassible if you want to stay dry, but otherwise perfectly passible.  Fjord the thing!  We didn’t play all those years of Oregon Trail for nothing.  This photo is from the return trip, but it gives you the idea

Crossing Bubbs Creek

To sum up: captain safety pants was concerned; I scouted for a rock or tree bridge, unsuccessfully; by the time I returned Alex had impetuously crossed and was lacing back his boots, an argument that spoke for itself.

The 3 miles to East Lake were constant uphill and deserted but for a few exceptions: the bear that almost ate Alex (but ended up being a deer); two summer hires busting rocks with hammers & pry bars; and a couple descending from East Lake who informed us the entire lake was ours.  The summer hires beg elaboration.  They work seasonally, from June through September, maintaining trails.  In this case, maintaining the trail meant moving several thousand pounds of rock to convert a knee-high step into two smaller steps.  Their tools were not sophisticated; they would be recognized by Virgil, perhaps by Homer: iron bars, hammers, and chisels.  They camp in Junction Meadow all summer, periodically resupplied by mule pack trains, and in October they pack out and find another job.  Not bad.

We ate just above the wooden bridge, on a shady rock near the rushing water.  The valley narrows as it ascends, and you find yourself on steep slopes among big trees, mostly Cedars I believe.  Smaller and younger than their 3000 year old Sequoia cousins in the western part of the park, they make you feel small nonetheless (note Jason & Bill on the trail).

East Lake Trail

We camped above the shores of East Lake, the valley obscured by afternoon smoke.  Jason swam in his improvised swimsuit in some very brisk water.  We made dinner.  John remembered to remove the natural clay dessicant.  We slept again under the starlight, filtered through pine boughs.

Morning dawned clear, cool, gentle, no sound but the distant flowing water.  The high stream at Junction Meadow had kept everyone else away.  We awoke to the serenity you seek in the mountains: 3 miles from the nearest people, 15 miles from the nearest road, pink morning sun on sharp granite peaks, reflected in a clear lake.  This is why I do it.

East Lake at dawn

East Lake at dawn

After some failures, Jason perfected pancakes for breakfast — not simple on a thin strip of titanium over a barely-adjustable flame.  I think he even had syrup.  We could have spent another day here in solitude, scaling the local peaks and exploring the higher streams & ponds.  Sublime.  But we were due out of Onion Valley the next day, so we agreed to a 10 mile day, -1300′ then +2700′, putting us at Kearsarge Lakes for our final night.  The hike down to Junction Meadow was easy: as John said, he was designed to walk downhill, not up.

Down East Creek

Up Bubbs Creek

Lunch at Vidette Meadow, and a rest in the shade beneath the granite faces of the Kearsarge Pinnacles and East Vidette Peak.  We were getting tired (maybe not Jason).  Next was the 1000′ ascent through the ferns and stream crossings on the John Muir trail.  Reaching the top was a relief.  We verged on giddy.

Near Bullfrog Lake

We finished the flat 2 miles to Kearsarge Lakes, debated bushwacking, split up, and ultimately noted that the bushwack saved a few hundred yards, if that.  I got a headache.  Everyone else seemed fine, though tired.  Our “kitchen” got closer yet to our beds (our motto: each night closer than the last!).  At dusk we watched the trout jump, hundreds of splashes per minute on the otherwise placid lake.  At 10900′ we lacked the oxygen to be serious.  Arnold Swartznegger made many appearances.  Also the Pleadies.  The cold chased us into sleeping bags early, again under the stars, but for Bill & Jason with lighter bags in a tent.  I barely slept; enjoyed those Pleadies and a bright wide milky way running nearly north-south (which confused me greatly, thinking at the time that our ecliptic is aligned with the Milky Way’s; the interwebs unconfused me later).  Bill & Jason thought they heard Alex engaged in unorthodox bear defenses.

The glow of dawn began imperceptibly in the east over Kearsarge Pass, our outbound route (last gap on the left)

Kearsarge Pass at dawn

Sun crept down the pinnacles and finally warmed our camp.

Oh descent, how nice.  We ate and swam at Gilbert Lake.  Very cold, very refreshing.  It’s hard to remember the end.  Back to the car, back to Lone Pine, John attracting the ladies with his face dirt.  Across Death Valley, back to Vegas, nice rooms, sort of an anti-East-Lake:  hundreds of thousands of people within sight of your window and a toilet that spares you the indignity of lifting the lid.

Venetian Bathroom

Next year we hope to return.
