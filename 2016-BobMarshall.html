Bob Marshall Wilderness
August 2016

Who is Bob Marshall? Why have I never heard of him or his wilderness? Where is this place? Queue the back story.

It begins with a genuinely useful publication of our general government: a map of distance to the nearest road in the lower 48 states (original pdf here, and as usual, click any photo for the full res version).

Courtesy of USGS

The algorithm is simple: find the biggest dark green spots we haven't visited. Future candidates include the Olympics & Cascades (WA), Frank Church (ID), and Yellowstone complex (WY). This year we chose the largest green blob in Montana, which happens to be called the Bob Marshall complex. The 1.5 million acre wilderness was named for an early conservationist.

Oddly-named wilderness areas remain obscure for reasons I don't understand but I do enjoy. Compared to national park backcountry, the scenery is comparable, the permits easier, the camping and fire rules friendlier, and the crowds smaller. For background, a wilderness designation prohibits roads, permanent structures, and mechanized travel, with few grandfathered exceptions. About 5% of the lower 48 states (110 million acres) are officially wilderness, in addition to other public lands that happen to be roadless.

If you think "The Bob" is a strange name, the naming gets even stranger. The Bob's marquee feature, a 1000 foot cliff that extends unbroken for 20 miles along the continental divide, is called the Chinese Wall. With a description and name like that, we had to see it.

Montana has truly big skies. The rolling prairies and mesas, dotted with more cattle and horses than people, surrender abruptly to forest at the foothills.

Near Augusta, MT

At one point we had to stop for three steer occupying the road, and those steer outnumbered the cars we saw in the hour between Augusta and Benchmark. The end of the road had a well-marked trailhead, a paucity of cars, and a luxurious outhouse.

I'm becoming increasingly partial to the northern Rockies. They have numerous rivers and green undergrowth. Their trails are wild and scenic but not too high or steep or dry for visitors from sea level carrying steaks, bacon, sausage, and eggs.

We entered along the Continental Divide Trail (CDT). It was gently rolling, well trodden, with several bridges, allowing rapid travel into the backcountry with modest exertion. The view from the first pack bridge, a few hundred yards from the car, was pure Montana.

Getting straight to business: the South Fork Sun

The trail goes north through shady forest, yielding to burn scars in the lower valley, yielding to open meadows west of the confluence.

Above the South Fork and West Fork confluence

It then follows the West Fork Sun, sometimes low in a meadow, sometimes high enough for a view.

Low in a meadow

High enough for a view

Thanks to our early-ish start we traveled 11 miles and made camp at 4pm just before the Ahorn trail junction. We had hours of sunshine to cook the steaks and spinach and assemble the fresh kale salad. We should strive to start every trip so early.

Backcountry kale

We felt great. I had no overexertion from a steep climb, no altitude problems (we were at 5400'; net gain of zero from the car, but about 1000' of up-and-down), and no dehydration. There was no wind sapping our heat. We had a campfire to cook ribeye and to keep us warm and social after sundown.

Dehydrated food is for chumps

Day Two - The skies cleared overnight, revealing modest stars and causing a chilly morning. Jason's breakfast burritos were as good as ever, with open fire bacon and pan-fried bacon, eggs (in the bacon grease), cheese, onion, and warm tortillas. Alex has achieved true mastery of campfire bacon.

We left camp at 10:10, immediately passed the Ahorn junction, shortly passed Indian Point, and soon reached White River Pass trail, which starts with an ice cold shoes-off crossing. The fire scars ended along this stretch and the forest grew greener and denser as we ascended. A thick clump of raspberries distracted us for 10 minutes.

The gathering instinct at work

We chased the raspberries with occasional trailside thimbleberries until lunch, which featured turkey pitas with cheese, avacado, and tomato. The forest opened up to the first vistas above Indian Creek.

Upper Indian Creek Valley

We left the trail at the final stream crossing (~7160') and followed an unofficial trail to the saddle above Red Butte Creek. Here we got our first glimpse of the 1000' cliffs of the Chinese Wall.

JC and about 1/8 of the Chinese Wall

We followed disappearing game trails across marshy ground at equal elevation until we reached the unnamed lake in the upper Red Butte valley, our site for the next two nights. There was bear poop in the middle of the optimal tenting area, but it was old enough to be unconcerning.

Padraig cooked a tomato pasta with pepperoni and delicious shitakes. We made another fire and stayed up late. I got the best night's camping sleep I've had in years; if this post doesn't grow too long I'll talk later about this year's experimental techniques.

Day Three - What a day! We awoke to this:

The view from my sleeping bag

Our stationary day supports a customary two hour endeavor to prepare and cook (and clean up from) pancakes and bacon, which is worth every minute. Bill salvaged the soupy batter with an amoeba/rorschach technique that achieved better browning than I achieve in a kitchen.

Amoeba cakes

We argued for an hour about day hike routes, with Alex wanting to see the Chinese Wall from its foot and JC wanting a high panoramic view. Our solution was the best of both worlds: we would circumnavigate the ridge that we named the Taiwanese Wall (southeast and smaller than the Chinese Wall). It was a tidy loop, entirely off trail, about the right length (4-5 hours), and offering both low and high perspectives on the wall. Bill, JC, Alex, and I were in; the Rogers and Suhas were relaxing.

The steepest and slowest portion was the initial 600' descent to Red Butte Creek. We avoided the few unhikeable grades by switching back up the valley. We passed through some huckleberry patches that foreshadowed serious grizzly habitat a bit later.

Down to Red Butte Creek

We crossed the creek and followed on-and-off game trails at roughly constant elevation (~6600') through increasingly prime bear territory. As we rounded the apex of the ridge we found copious huckleberries, disturbed soil, frequent scat, and fresh tracks. I would not want to hike this portion alone.

Bear country

Several years ago in Glacier we found huge fields of huckleberries. This valley put even those to shame: we walked for over an hour through nearly-continuous huckleberries, tangy and sweet, so many huckleberries that the bears pooped purple-blue:

Huckleberries to the limit

As we hiked upstream, the gaps in the forest revealed the grandeur of the Chinese Wall. It was definitely worth seeing from below.

Chinese Wall from below

For the next hour, each opening was more stunning than the last. We stopped at one such vista for lunch: the no-cleanup, easily subdividable, yet delicious, gouda, gruyere, pepperoni, and crackers. Shorly afterward we found this spot at about 6800' that would make a great campsite (except for the early sunsets).

More Chinese Wall

The valley soon climbs above the treeline.

Hiking higher

An easily hikeable grade reached our exit pass (~8200'), which sits at the southern terminus of the Chinese Wall, NNW of White River Pass. The scenery was superb to the horizon in all directions. And despite walking several hours along the Wall's footing, we had seen only about a third of its total length, the northern portion being occluded by the protrusion at Cliff Mountain.

Posing at our high point, the southern terminus of the Chinese Wall

It took less than an hour to traverse and descend to our campsite, which is the lake midway up the right slope.

Home sweet home in sight

We arrived at camp, greated the relaxers, and swam very briefly in the pockets of sunshine. The perceived temperature varied by 20 degrees (?) between sun and shade: when sunny, comfortable though wet and nearly naked; when shady, wanting a jacket though dry and fully clothed.

After Jambalaya dinner -- well proportioned with old chicken and moldy sausage -- we built a respectable fire and burned a recalcitrant log in half until 11pm. Roger sowed confusion about the exit hike, and he and Alex debated the trail running time to the car.

At bedtime, inspired by a prior conversation with dchen, I made my first ever attempt at nighttime photography. On my own monitor its clarity depends on brightness, ambient light, and viewing angle. I rate it passable, given that it is a first attempt with a six-year-old point-and-shoot camera (the next night's photo, further below, is a little better).

Day Four -- We had three options for the day: a backtrack of day two, a backtrack all the way to the car, and a partial loop exit through Ahorn and Hoadley valleys. Roger lobbied for the full exit, though with mischievous conviction. We harbored some suspicion that Roger had not actually brought the fourth night's dinner. Alex and Jason and I entertained the Ahorn loop, though the extra length -- an extra 8+ miles and 2000'+, by our best estimate -- was intimidating. The chia oatmeal cooked & cleaned quickly, and amidst intrigue and accusations of sandbagging we began at 9:30am, just as the sun crested the ridge.

The return passed quickly without the novelty of new scenery, especially since it was downhill. Here is the upper part of the descent, this time traveling east and down the valley towards Prairie Reef.

Indian Creek towards Prairie Reef

We passed a few slabs that explained the "reef" nomenclature -- they appear to be rippled like the sea. I don't know enough geology to say whether rippling water was actually involved or not.

Some "reef" rocks (?)

We lunched at the Ahorn junction. My tuna sandwiches, at 5.5 oz tuna per person, plus mayo, bread, and sides, were way too dense and heavy, even at our relatively low elevation. I was accused of sandbagging again. No more tuna in the future.

We were too tired to attempt the Ahorn loop, which at minimum would have meant an afternoon of 8+ miles and 2000'+. So we continued retracing our inbound journey. After another few hours, with eveyone feeling weary, Jason announced a decent campsite, which we gladly accepted. Though the day was mostly on trail and downhill, the 13 miles (per gps) were tiring. Roger's full exit was not to be.

I was too lazy to take photos of our camp. We slowly setup tents, took foot baths in the icy river, and as the winds grew gusty we cooked Roger's pesto, which he did in fact possess.

The winds, though gusty, were extremely warm -- so warm it was weird. Even past 10pm, with strong breezes, it was pleasant to sit in a t-shirt. We sweated through the night, and I'd be surprised if it got below 70. I believe we encountered a mild version of the Chinook Winds, a Montana phenomenon that once created a record 24-hour temperature swing (+100 degree swing in 24 hours).

The evening had several highlights. One was the warmth, allowing comfortable lounging late into our final evening. One was the book discussion while lounging. One was the sky, brighter and clearer than any night yet, evoking thoughts of the centuries in which man had stood in that spot looking at those stars, and which I captured in a slightly improved nighttime photo session:

When adjusted to see the tree silhouettes, you see what we saw
