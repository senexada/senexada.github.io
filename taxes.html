Tax Year 2015

I learned that:

Partnerships have tax complications, including super-tax rates

The downsides:

  • K-1 Timing -- often arrive after April 15. This year I got a corrected K1 in October
  • Mgmt Fee -- is not deductible, so creates a super-tax rate
  • State taxes -- can create non-resident tax liability in many states

Super-tax example: a net return of 10%, based on a 12% return of operating income and a 2% management fee. The income is taxed at 50.5% (see calculations in 2014), so the tax is $6.06 for every $10 in net return. If the 12% is a capital gain, taxed at 33%, the tax is $3.96 for every $10 in net return: nearly 40%.

In fairness, some advantages of partnerships include privacy, majority control, no double taxation, different opportunities than public markets (smaller companies, etc), and access to non-recourse leverage (a dubious advantage; I should write a leverage analysis).

Mark-to-Market Assets Are Doubly Bad

Consider an asset that gains 30% over 3 years, but with volatility. As an example, 
  Year 1 =  +20%
  Year 2 =  +20%
  Year 3 =  -10%

Assume you are in a high bracket in a high-tax locale like nyc. You invest $1000 in asset A, which has mark-to-market 60/40 LT/ST treatment, or asset B, which does not.

In a simple case, you sell the asset at the end of year 3 while still in your high tax locale. We are using 50.5% and 33% for ST and LT rates (see 2014 notes below). 

  Asset A
  Year     LT Gain  LT Tax  ST Gain  ST Tax
  ====     =======  ======  =======  ======
   1       200*0.6    40    200*0.4    40
   2       240*0.6    48    240*0.4    48
   3       -144*0.6  -29   -144*0.4   -29

   TOTAL              59              59   = 118 tax

  Asset B
  Year     LT Gain  LT Tax  ST Gain  ST Tax
  ====     =======  ======  =======  ======
   1          0       0        0        0
   2          0       0        0        0
   3         300     100       0        0

   TOTAL             100                   = 100 tax

On its face, the tax difference is small: $18 on the $300 gain, or 6%. But the situation can deteriorate in many ways. If you can hold asset B until in a low income year and locale, or if you donate B, you could reduce B's tax as far as $0. In that case, the tax rate difference is 39% vs. 0%.

An additional hit can arise if you have other LT gains. In the case that A's year 3 ST losses offset LT gains, the tax benefit of -29 is reduced to -19. Thus A's effective tax rate becomes 43%, verses B's effective rate of 0-33%.

Tax Year 2014

I learned that:

Lending Club income can be taxed at 70% or more

Impossible, you say?

Say your account grew in value by $1000. One possible breakdown (similar to my actual breakdown one year) is +2000 interest income and -1000 loan chargeoffs.

The +2000 is fully taxed at your max rate, which in nyc can be as high as 39.6 + 3.8 + 8.97 + 3.5 = 55.87% (it's actually a bit less because the feds don't tax you on your state tax. So, it's more like 50.5%) The -1000 is a capital loss, which offsets long term cap gains at 20 + 3.8 + 8.97 + 3.5 = 36.27% (actually 33%, for the same reasons above). 

   Tax on income = 2000 * 0.505 = 1010
   Tax reduction from capital loss = 1000 * 0.33 = 330
   Net taxes from Lending Club = 670

Your net income was 1000 and you paid 670 in extra taxes --> 67% tax rate. It can be worse if you already have net capital losses, in which case the capital loss benefit cannot be used until a future year. Depending on the time until used, your choice of discount rate, and your locale/income when used, the capital loss "benefit" could be worth as little as zero (if your income is low enough to be in the 0% federal capital gains bracket and you live in a no-income-tax state). In that worst case -- admittedly, an extreme scenario -- the Lending Club income was taxed at over 100% !
