The Arrival

I arrived Fresno 8pm with only carry-on luggage (first time attempting it; simply gave up on packing a knife). Bill picked me up, we slept in Fresno, and were driving north to Wawona at 624a on Thu Oct 3.

Along the way we discussed my new equipment this year: the KS Ultralight pack. It weighs one pound and has a minimal frame. Way lighter than an REI pack, just as comfortable, yet also cheaper than REI, despite being custom hand-sown by a sole proprietor in one of the costliest places on earth (Japan). Which begs the big-box retail question explored by Flight of the Conchords in Think About It: "What are your overheads?"

What are your overheads?

The ranger station at Wawona Hotel was empty (arriving half hour after they opened, we dodged the 8-person opening rush). The ranger surprised us with his bear advice, so different than the northern rockies: sleep *near* your bear cans; act aggressively toward bears, as if the bear is a big raccoon; but don't stone them to death, which accidentally happened in a Lord of the Flies frenzy recently (oops). He also surprised us with the forecast: close to 100 degrees at Hetch Hetchy.

A juvenile bear skittered across the parking lot as we arrived at White Wolf at 10a. We took our starting photo and began the dirt road walk toward Harden Lake.

White Wolf parking lot

After passing Harden Lake (not particularly scenic), we admired the trailsmanship of the switchbacks down the south rim of the Grand Canyon of the Tuolumne. The first few miles had no veiws; at one point we got a few glimpses of the deep blue waters of Hetch Hetchy Reservoir; as we got lower, the views pointed toward Pate Valley and Rancheria Mountain.

Hetch Hetchy from afar

Rancheria Mountain

It was an unusual backcountry entry: a net 3500' of elevation *loss* as we descended from the Tioga Road highlands into the furnace of Tuolumne canyon. Along the canyon floor we took multiple breaks to soak our shirts in the river.

Soaking shirt in the Tuolumne

We crossed the two good bridges in Pate Valley and found a shady campsite in the tall pines. It was dark by 7:15p, which foreshadows our biggest challenge of this October trip: staying awake until a reasonable bedtime.

Campsite in Pate Valley
