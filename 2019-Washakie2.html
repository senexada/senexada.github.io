Day Three

There is something soothing about the multi-hour pancake ritual on the stationary day. While Alex searched for a swimming hole in the untainted wilderness, Steve and I assembled a river-rock tandoor as a potential pizza oven.

Construction

Wilderness oven

About 1pm, Jason and I departed to explore Jeff's Buttress (as noted in last night's campsite photo) and beyond. It did not disappoint.

We reached the buttress itself in just over an hour of steep hiking with a few scrambles. To the south and east, we saw our campsite and tomorrow's route over the unnamed pass at the Wiggins headwaters. (You can barely see our tents in the high res version, near the river)

Jeff's Buttress, looking up Wiggins Fork

To the northeast was Burwell Pass, with yesterday's trail zagging down the mountainside.

Yesterday's trail down from Burwell Pass

And looking west and northwest, the crow can fly 60+ miles before seeing a road. Just past the midpoint of that flight, near Hawk's Rest in the Thoroughfare region of Yellowstone, is the farthest point from a road in the lower 48 states.

Though remote, the Thoroughfare is a busy area of Yellowstone, popular with outfitters. Many summer days register 100+ people and 200+ mules past the ranger cabin [1]. We, in contrast, saw no one for five days in these marvelously scenic adjacent lands.

Looking down Wiggins Fork toward distant Yellowstone

Continuing upward from Jeff's Buttress was a delightful, meanindering ridge stroll. We were circled by Ospreys and eyed by leery elk. This area is so remote than many of the peaks, streams, and valleys are unnamed. I was particularly attracted to the unnamed valleys southwest of Mount Burwell, which may make an excellent future destination.

But thanks to 2.5 hours of clever pan management , Suhas and his sous chef delivered seven pizzas of superlative quality. It was such visually appealing food in such a wonderful setting that I will break my usual prohibition of millenial-style food pics.

Day Four

My water bottle was frozen solid at dawn. We packed, ate quickly, and were hiking within ten minutes of our target time of 9:30. The hike followed Wiggins fork uphill at a gentle slope, up and over an unnamed nearly-11k' pass.

Looking back as we ascend Wiggins Fork

Looking forward down the next valley (note us on trail in foreground)

Does hiking get better? Massive green valley after green valley, clear skies, near-continuous 360-degree views, not a soul, and so seldom visited that large features remain unnamed and the trail frequently vanishes.

The Absoroka Range (pronounced Ab-sork-a) is igneous, which I feared might be black, ugly, and monotonous. The opposite was true. Grasses & lichen cover the hillsides far above the treeline. The ridges are rounded, presumably due to more rapid erosion. Together these features make for excellent off-trail travel. Many of the ridges to our left (along the Park / Fremont County boundary) looked hikeable from this side, in particular the two valleys north of Dollar Mountain. The hanging valleys to our right (west) look aluring and worth exploring. Future off-trail travel through any of these areas may be excellent.

We had some trail confusion as the Absaroka trail leaves Caldwell Creek and turns southeast. Within 40 minutes we had found the trail, hiked above a gorge with waterfalls and beautiful pools, and stopped for lunch at the first crossing of the unnamed stream. This short stretch was the least scenic of the trip -- weird topography, limited views, and dead trees -- and thus I lack worthwhile photos.

What I don't lack are notes about lunch. The sandbagging discussions intensified as Roger sowed his usual confusion about an early exit. There were numerous factors this year: shortcuts over the unnamed passes; losing the trail (was it intentional?); availability of hotel rooms; Steve's nalgene malfunction that soaked his down sleeping bag; length of bathroom breaks; shifting alliances. To foreshadow the result, I had learned my lesson from prior trips and had carefully chosen a route that required extreme mileage to exit early.

The unnamed creek continued up the most stark, lunar-like scenery of the trip, with lava rocks and little vegetation. Near the top of the valley was a striking peach-colored mountainside, but the valley was so cramped I couldn't fit the entire mountain into the photo.

A striking hillside

Beyond the unnamed pass was, you guessed it, another miles-wide, beautiful valley, the Bear Creek drainage.

Bear Creek Drainage from unnamed pass

Looking back toward unnamed pass and the peach mountain

Kind of backlit, but worth including

The hike down was really neat. Suhas compared the look-and-feel to Lamar Valley in Yellowstone. The trail often disappeared completely in the rolling grasslands. Apparently the stream descent gets cliffy, but we stayed high & left and followed the wooden posts (spaced several hundred yards apart and sometimes missing or hard to spot) along the correct route. Across the valley we spied a herd of elk, our best count being 57. Spooked by us, we watched them run in five minutes a distance that would take us half an hour.

Descending to Bear Creek (note us in bottom left)

Elk on the other side of Bear Creek

After an hour we reached the valley floor and briefly considered stopping. It was past 4pm, we had gone 8.3 miles, and no one was particularly energetic. However, the 900' up and over Bear Creek Pass had to be done one way or another, and doing it today would make for an earlier exit and meetup with Padraig. In fact, it was possible Padraig would be hiking up toward us and we could meet him in the home stretch along Wood River.

Up Bear Creek

The trail followed the Bear Creek stream bed and was hard to discern. Mostly we walked in the bed itself. At Bear Creek Pass (our final 11k' pass) the wind briefly subsided, giving us a few ridge-top minutes of quiet serenity. We descended the steep mountainside into the long, peaceful shadows of approaching sunset.

Toward final camp

We camped at the first flat spot along Wood River, where Steve had to frighten off grazing cattle. In our weariness, and the now-resumed wind, it took a surprisingly long time to cook pesto pasta.

Night four camp, Wood River

Umm, is it still not ready? Nope.

Padraig had not hiked up to join us, but no worries, he was probably spending one more night in a hotel and would meet us in the morning at the parking lot. Or so we assumed.

Day Five

We began stirring at 6:30am and were on trail at 8:04. Suhas achieved super speed on the packed dirt, and the river crossings this late August were all hoppable. Within two hours we passed a fallen gate with sign stating "Administrative Use. No camping", though we didn't know exactly how to interpret or circumscribe that command. Then we passed the ruins of Kirwin (an abandoned mining town) and reached the parking lot, having seen no one the entire trip, parking lot to parking lot. That's a first.

But the parking lot wasn't exactly how we left it. It now had a dilapidated RV, a pit bull, and Padraig staring us down with a "be cool, don't ask questions" stare. Oh, the things we learned on the roadtrip back to civilization. I can only summarize the contours, the details being inappropriate for this forum.

Padraig had decided to camp at the trailhead last night, and was pitching his tent as the RV sputtered in. P made the usual peace offerings and got immediate respect and street cred for camping in a tent, given, the men said, the active male grizzly in the area. Hmm. They shared some colorful life stories, the mildest being how the RV was purchased for $500, which included spare tires they sold to recoup some of the cost (when prior vehicles got stuck, they just abandoned them and walked on).

As the campfire-beside-the-RV dwindled, having consumed all the trash, metal, and plastic thrown into it, Padraig warily began his night in the tent. And, wouldn't you know, the guys who spent days traveling through prime bear habitat saw nothing, while Padraig was close enough, in dead of night, to hear a grizzly breathing. I don't know how he kept his composure.

The pit bull, in the hard-sided RV, was apparently going nuts. When they let him outside, he spied the grizzly and ran back cowering to be let inside. The commotion, barks, shouts, and honking (from the RV) scared it away. I can't imagine sleeping another wink after that. I guess this is what the ranger meant when he had laconicly described Kirwin as "there's bears up there."

As we finished packing our cars, the men, toting shovels, a sidearm, and with their pit bull, were heading up into the mountains for the hobby they pursued, summer & winter, rain or shine, on days off: searching for buried treasure.

Success. Survived the hike and the grizzly
